The mineral foundation. Kosher for cooking, fleur de sel for finishing. Understanding salt is understanding flavor.

Salt Types

Kosher Salt

Large granules, easy to pinch. No additives. Use for cooking. Called "kosher" because it's used to cure meat for Jewish dietary law, not because it's more pure.

Table Salt

Fine granules, often with anti-caking agents. Avoid. Too processed and tastes metallic. Use kosher salt instead.

Fleur de Sel

Hand-harvested, delicate. Expensive. Use for finishing ‚Äî the last thing on the plate. The crystals add texture and mineral brightness.

Sea Salt (Coarse)

Natural, large crystals. Use for cooking pasta water, brining. Similar to kosher salt.

How to Season Properly

Season as You Cook, Not at the End

Salt dissolves and distributes evenly if added during cooking. Add at the end and it sits on the surface, creating salty bites. Season at the beginning and middle stages.

Use Your Fingers

Pinching salt with your fingers gives you control over quantity and helps you develop intuition about how much you're adding. A three-finger pinch is approximately ¬Ω teaspoon.

Taste Constantly

You can always add more salt. You can't remove it. Under-salted food tastes flat. Properly salted food tastes bright and alive. Find the balance.

Finishing Salt Changes Everything

A tiny pinch of fleur de sel on a finished plate adds complexity and mineral character. The crystals don't dissolve ‚Äî they burst on the tongue. Use it on soups, salads, and roasted vegetables.
